## Methods Summary

The analysis employed the Wisconsin Breast Cancer dataset sourced from the scikit-learn library, comprising 569 samples described by 30 numerical features derived from digitized images of fine needle aspirate (FNA) biopsies of breast masses. The binary classification task distinguished malignant from benign tumors, with class labels distributed as 212 malignant and 357 benign observations, reflecting a moderate degree of class imbalance. The dataset was partitioned into a training set of 455 samples and a held-out test set of 114 samples, with the test set containing 42 malignant and 72 benign cases. Two classification algorithms were evaluated: logistic regression and random forest. Model selection was conducted using stratified 5-fold cross-validation optimized on the area under the receiver operating characteristic curve (ROC AUC), ensuring that class proportions were preserved across folds. Feature importance was assessed using the mean decrease in impurity derived from the random forest model, providing a ranked contribution of each input variable to predictive performance.

Both preprocessing and hyperparameter configurations were held at their default settings with a fixed random seed of 42, ensuring reproducibility. The stratified cross-validation procedure was chosen deliberately to account for the unequal distribution of malignant and benign cases, thereby producing unbiased estimates of generalization performance. Final model evaluation was performed on the held-out test set, with performance characterized by accuracy, ROC AUC, precision, recall, F1 score, and the full confusion matrix. This dual-evaluation strategy, combining cross-validated training performance with a separate test set assessment, provided a more robust characterization of model behavior than single-split evaluation alone, though the relatively modest test set size of 114 samples warrants some caution in interpreting point estimates.

## Model Comparison

Both logistic regression and random forest demonstrated strong discriminative performance on this dataset, with logistic regression emerging as the superior model according to cross-validated ROC AUC. Logistic regression achieved a mean cross-validation AUC of 0.9958 (SD = 0.0047), with individual fold AUCs ranging from 0.9871 to 1.0000, indicating consistently high and stable performance across data splits. Random forest yielded a slightly lower mean cross-validation AUC of 0.9889 (SD = 0.0070), with fold-level performance ranging from 0.9833 to 1.0000, and a marginally greater variance across folds. On the held-out test set, both models achieved identical accuracy of 96.49%, underscoring the competitiveness of the two approaches on this particular data partition.

Despite equivalent accuracy, the two models exhibited a meaningful trade-off in precision and recall on the test set. Logistic regression produced a precision of 0.975 and recall of 0.929, yielding an F1 score of 0.951, and its confusion matrix recorded one false positive and three false negatives among the 114 test cases. Random forest achieved a perfect precision of 1.000, generating no false positives, but at the cost of slightly reduced recall of 0.905, corresponding to four false negatives and an F1 score of 0.950. In the clinical context of malignancy detection, false negatives carry substantially greater risk than false positives, as a missed malignancy may delay potentially life-saving treatment. From this perspective, logistic regression's marginally higher recall—correctly identifying one additional malignant case at the expense of one benign misclassification—may confer a practical advantage, and its designation as the best model based on cross-validated AUC of 0.9958 versus 0.9889 was consistent with this outcome.

## Feature Interpretation

The random forest feature importance rankings revealed that morphological characteristics describing the size and shape of the tumor's boundary were the most informative predictors of malignancy. The top-ranked feature was worst perimeter (importance = 0.148), followed closely by worst area (0.132) and worst concave points (0.110), where "worst" denotes the mean of the three largest values recorded for a given nuclear characteristic across the FNA image. These size-related features are biologically consistent with known histological markers of malignancy: cancerous cells typically exhibit nuclear enlargement, irregular contours, and increased mitotic activity, all of which manifest as larger and more geometrically irregular boundaries when quantified from digitized biopsy images. The prominence of worst-statistic features across the top rankings suggests that the most extreme nuclear measurements, rather than average measurements alone, carried particular discriminative signal.

Concavity-related features also featured prominently, with worst concave points (0.110), mean concave points (0.088), mean concavity (0.044), and worst concavity (0.033) all appearing among the top ten contributors. Concave points and concavity capture the number and severity of concave portions of the cell nucleus contour, features that reflect the lobulated or irregular nuclear shape characteristic of malignant cells. The co-occurrence of both mean and worst variants of radius, perimeter, area, and concavity features in the importance rankings suggests that malignancy signal was distributed across multiple summary statistics of the same underlying morphological constructs, consistent with the known redundancy among the 30 features in this dataset. Together, these findings reinforce an established biomedical understanding: nuclear geometry, particularly the scale and irregularity of cell contours as captured through FNA, provides highly informative cues for distinguishing malignant from benign breast masses.

## Limitations

Several methodological limitations constrain the interpretive scope of these findings. Most critically, the analysis was conducted on a single, well-curated benchmark dataset of 569 samples, and no external validation on independent patient cohorts was performed. The Wisconsin Breast Cancer dataset is a widely used academic resource that has been extensively analyzed, and models trained on it may benefit implicitly from its well-structured properties, potentially overstating the generalizability of the observed performance to heterogeneous clinical populations with varying imaging protocols, patient demographics, or biopsy procedures. The relatively small test set of 114 samples introduces non-trivial uncertainty in point estimates of precision, recall, and F1, particularly given that only 42 malignant cases were available for evaluation; a single additional false negative, for instance, would reduce recall from 0.929 to 0.905 for logistic regression, substantially altering the apparent clinical trade-off between the two models.

Beyond dataset limitations, the analysis did not incorporate hyperparameter optimization, class-imbalance correction techniques such as oversampling or cost-sensitive learning, or calibration assessment of predicted probabilities, all of which are relevant considerations in a clinical decision-support context. Feature importance derived from random forest mean decrease in impurity is known to exhibit bias toward high-cardinality continuous features and does not account for feature interactions or redundancy, which is particularly relevant given the strong collinearity among size-related morphological descriptors in this dataset. Furthermore, the two-class framing of the task, while appropriate for this dataset, abstracts away important clinical nuances such as tumor grade, receptor status, and stage, which are integral to actual breast cancer diagnosis and management. Any prospective clinical application of these models would necessitate rigorous multi-site validation, prospective study design, and integration with broader clinical context before conclusions about diagnostic utility could responsibly be drawn.
