## Methods Summary

The analysis employed the Wisconsin Breast Cancer dataset, a widely used benchmark derived from digitized fine needle aspirate (FNA) images of breast masses. The dataset comprised 569 samples in total, with 455 samples allocated to training and 114 to testing using a fixed random seed (42) to ensure reproducibility. Of the 569 cases, 212 were labeled malignant and 357 benign, yielding a moderate class imbalance with benign cases representing approximately 62.7% of the dataset. Each sample was characterized by 30 numerical features computed from cell nucleus measurements, encompassing ten primary measurements — including radius, texture, perimeter, area, smoothness, compactness, concavity, concave points, symmetry, and fractal dimension — each represented as a mean, standard error, and worst (most extreme) value across cell nuclei in the image.

Two classifiers were trained and evaluated: a logistic regression model with standardized inputs via a StandardScaler, and a Random Forest ensemble classifier. Model selection was performed using stratified 5-fold cross-validation, with the mean area under the receiver operating characteristic curve (ROC AUC) serving as the primary evaluation metric. Stratification ensured that the malignant-to-benign ratio was preserved across all folds, mitigating the potential distorting effect of class imbalance on fold-level estimates. Final performance was assessed on the held-out test set of 114 samples, with accuracy, AUC, precision, recall, and F1 score computed for each model.

---

## Model Comparison

During stratified 5-fold cross-validation, logistic regression achieved a mean ROC AUC of 0.9958 (SD = 0.0047), slightly outperforming the Random Forest, which recorded a mean AUC of 0.9889 (SD = 0.0070). The logistic regression also demonstrated greater consistency across folds, with a lower standard deviation indicating reduced sensitivity to the specific composition of individual training folds. The Random Forest exhibited marginally wider fold-to-fold variation, with individual fold AUCs ranging from 0.9833 to 1.0000, compared to a range of 0.9871 to 1.0000 for logistic regression. Based on these cross-validation results, logistic regression was designated the best-performing model.

On the held-out test set, both models achieved identical overall accuracy of 96.49%, yet they diverged in the composition of their errors. Logistic regression attained a test AUC of 0.9960, a precision of 0.975, and a recall of 0.929 for the malignant class, yielding an F1 score of 0.951. The Random Forest achieved a slightly lower test AUC of 0.9942 and a perfect precision of 1.0, but a reduced recall of 0.905 and F1 score of 0.950. In the context of breast cancer screening, where failing to detect a malignant case carries greater clinical consequence than a false positive, the higher recall of logistic regression (0.929 vs. 0.905) represents a meaningful practical advantage. Despite producing six false negatives on the test set, logistic regression misclassified fewer malignant cases than the Random Forest, which produced eight false negatives while committing no false positives.

## Feature Interpretation

Feature importance derived from the Random Forest model via Gini impurity reduction identified morphological size and shape irregularity measurements, particularly their worst-case values, as the most discriminative features for distinguishing malignant from benign tumors. The single most important feature was worst perimeter (importance = 0.148), followed closely by worst area (0.132) and worst concave points (0.110). These three features alone accounted for approximately 39% of the total impurity-based importance, suggesting that the extreme values of nuclear boundary length, nuclear area, and the count of concave portions of the nuclear contour were collectively the strongest indicators of malignancy in this dataset.

Mean-level features also contributed substantially to model decisions. Mean concave points ranked fourth in importance (0.088), followed by worst radius (0.085), mean radius (0.061), and mean perimeter (0.060). The consistent prominence of size-related features — radius, perimeter, and area at both mean and worst levels — alongside concavity and concave points reflects the known pathological pattern in which malignant nuclei tend to be larger, more irregular, and more concave than benign ones. Features characterizing the worst-case measurements accounted for five of the top ten features and collectively dominated importance scores, indicating that extreme cellular abnormality, rather than average cellular morphology alone, was most predictive of malignancy in this sample. It should be noted that Gini impurity-based importance can overweight continuous and high-cardinality features and does not provide the same inferential precision as model coefficients from a calibrated logistic regression.

## Limitations

Several limitations constrain the generalizability of the findings reported here. The analysis was conducted on a single, well-curated benchmark dataset of 569 samples, and no external validation cohort was employed. Performance estimates derived from the stratified 5-fold cross-validation and the held-out test set therefore reflect behavior on this specific dataset and cannot be taken as reliable indicators of how either model would perform on data collected from different institutions, imaging equipment, or patient populations. The Wisconsin Breast Cancer dataset is also a digitized benchmark that has been extensively studied and pre-cleaned, and it does not capture the noise, missingness, or distributional heterogeneity characteristic of real-world clinical data.

The class imbalance present in the dataset — with benign cases outnumbering malignant cases at a ratio of approximately 1.68 to 1 — was addressed through stratified sampling but not through any resampling or cost-sensitive learning strategy.
