## Executive Summary

Differential expression analysis was performed on the *Homo sapiens* airway dataset (Himes et al., 2014; PMID 24926665) to characterize transcriptomic changes associated with dexamethasone treatment in primary human airway smooth muscle cells. A total of 16,596 genes were evaluated using a paired experimental design comprising four cell lines assayed under treated and untreated conditions. Of these, 4,099 genes were identified as statistically significantly differentially expressed following adjustment for multiple comparisons, representing approximately 24.7% of genes tested. Among these, 2,201 were upregulated and 1,898 were downregulated in the treated relative to the untreated condition, indicating a broad and relatively balanced transcriptional response associated with glucocorticoid exposure. Principal component analysis revealed that PC1 and PC2 captured 48% and 24% of total variance, respectively, suggesting that treatment condition was a dominant source of transcriptomic variation in this dataset.

The scale of the observed response is consistent with the well-characterized pleiotropic transcriptional activity of glucocorticoid receptor signaling, which regulates gene expression across diverse functional categories including immune modulation, cytoskeletal organization, and metabolic processes. The paired design employed across four independent cell lines strengthens the interpretability of the statistical findings by controlling for inter-individual variability inherent to primary cell culture systems.

## Results Interpretation

Among the five most statistically significant differentially expressed genes, DUSP1 (dual specificity phosphatase 1; log2FC = 2.95, padj = 1.22 × 10⁻¹²⁴) exhibited the smallest effect size of the group yet is among the most functionally contextualized, as it is an established glucocorticoid-responsive gene whose upregulation has been previously documented in response to corticosteroid treatment. Its statistically significant upregulation in this dataset is concordant with the existing literature and provides internal validation of the experimental system. SAMHD1 (log2FC = 3.76, padj = 1.88 × 10⁻¹²⁴) and MAOA (log2FC = 3.35, padj = 2.57 × 10⁻¹¹⁹) also exhibited large effect sizes alongside exceptional statistical significance; however, the functional relevance of their differential expression in the context of airway smooth muscle glucocorticoid response warrants further investigation before biological conclusions can be drawn.

SPARCL1, a secreted extracellular matrix-associated protein, displayed the largest effect size among the top five genes (log2FC = 4.57, padj = 3.58 × 10⁻¹³²), while CACNB2, a voltage-gated calcium channel auxiliary subunit, showed a log2FC of 3.29 with comparable statistical significance. It should be noted that large log2 fold changes, while notable, do not independently establish biological importance; the functional significance of these associations requires corroboration through orthogonal experimental approaches. All five leading genes were upregulated in the treated condition, though this directional enrichment among top-ranked genes should not be interpreted as indicative of a globally upregulation-biased response, given the near-comparable counts of upregulated (2,201) and downregulated (1,898) genes across the full significant gene set.

## Limitations

Several limitations inherent to this analysis should be acknowledged. The dataset comprises four biological replicates per condition, which, while appropriate for a paired design and sufficient to achieve statistical power for the observed effect sizes, may limit the detection of genes with smaller or more variable expression differences. The analysis as reported does not include independent biological validation, such as quantitative PCR or protein-level measurements; consequently, all findings should be regarded as associative and hypothesis-generating rather than confirmatory. Furthermore, the transcriptomic changes described here reflect steady-state mRNA abundance following 18 hours of 1 µM dexamethasone exposure and cannot be directly extrapolated to other concentrations, time points, or cell types without additional experimentation.

The observational nature of RNA-seq data precludes causal inference: while 4,099 genes were found to be statistically significantly differentially expressed in association with dexamethasone treatment, these results do not establish that dexamethasone directly regulates any given gene, nor do they address post-transcriptional, translational, or protein-level consequences. Additionally, the analysis was conducted on primary airway smooth muscle cells cultured in vitro, and the degree to which these findings generalize to in vivo airway physiology or to disease-relevant contexts such as asthma remains an open question requiring further study.
